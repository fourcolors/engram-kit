# Adversarial Debate — How Much Data Does the n=1 Memory Benchmark Need?

## Setup

Two experts argue methodology for the Syke n=1 memory benchmark. The current packet is **19 probes × 3 conditions × 4 judge reps = 228 verdicts per judge family**, collected over one 15-day window (Apr 17–21, 2026). Measurements in play: `σ_ε^cell = 0.1836` (GPT) / `0.1873` (Opus), cross-judge shift `+0.5694` on the clean arm, verdict-asymmetry `22/54` opus-strictly-higher cells (and `0/54` reverse), rubric R² `0.754` (GPT) / `0.862` (Opus), `−0.011` drop-the-hero-axis ablation. **PARSIMONY** argues this is close to sufficient if estimated carefully; **RIGOR** argues the variance-decomposition the project wrote down is literally unidentified at this n and no cleverness fixes that. Both cite from the in-corpus papers at `papers/nondeterministic_verifier_202604/`. The stake: does the next cycle collect *more* probes, *deeper* reps on existing probes, or *different-time* asks?

---

## Round 1 — What is enough?

### PARSIMONY

The question isn't "is n=19 enough in the abstract," it's "is n=19 enough *for the effect size we've already measured*." We have `σ_γ = +0.5694` on a 0–2 ordinal scale and `σ_ε^cell = 0.1836`. The judge-identity shift is ~3.1× the per-cell flicker SD. Gelman's rule — the n you need is determined by the effect-to-noise ratio you're trying to resolve — says at d ≈ 3 you need ~10 paired observations for a two-sided test at α = 0.05 with 80% power, not 100. We have 45 common clean cells and the `22/54` strictly-higher asymmetry (CyclicJudge 2603.01865 §3.1 calls this "judge main effect"; Table 2 of that paper reports `σ̂_γ² = 0.34–0.95` across judge-model pairs on MT-Bench — our +0.57 sits squarely in-band). Autorubric (2603.00077) validated CHARM-100 at **100 items total**, not thousands; RiceChem is 1240 but it's population-scale chemistry grading, not n=1 memory. n=19 × 4 reps × 2 judges = 152 cells of agreement data per arm — that's not "dangerously small," that's a *moderately-powered* paired ordinal study for the claims we are actually making.

### RIGOR

PARSIMONY slid from "effect we measured" to "effect we can claim." The +0.5694 is a *point estimate* of σ_γ's mean shift on 19 probes. Plug 19 into CyclicJudge's own decomposition (their Eq. 2): `Var(X̄) = σ_α²/n + σ_β²/(nm) + σ_ε²/(nmK) + σ_γ² · FPC`. With n=19, m=1, K=4, the `σ_α²/n` term is *the largest* at any realistic σ_α — and we haven't estimated σ_α *at all* because MATH_ONE_PAGER §6 says it plainly: "α (probe) and β (agent) are never computed." Feuer 2025 (2509.20293) Fig. 1 shows DeepSeek-R1-32B had **87–90% unexplained variance** on Arena-Hard-Auto (n=500), and their whole schematic-adherence analytic *needed* 500 items × 4 judges to land. We have 19 items. Rao 2026 (2603.00077) calls rubric reliability measurable via Cohen's κ — but their RiceChem required 1240 responses × 27 criteria × 3 few-shot reps because you need ≥~30 items per criterion for α_Cronbach to stabilize. PARSIMONY's "paired ordinal test at d=3" is true-and-irrelevant: the claim the project *wants* to make isn't "judges differ," it's "architecture A beats architecture B on memory reconstruction." For *that* claim, n=19 is not moderate. It is underpowered by an order of magnitude.

## Round 2 — Noise decomposition math

### PARSIMONY

RIGOR conflates two claims. Yes, σ_α is uncomputed at n=19 — no argument. But the variance-decomposition math in CyclicJudge (2603.01865 §3.1, Eq. 1: `X_{ijℓ} = μ + α_i + β_{ij} + γ_ℓ + ε_{ijℓ}`) is estimable at this n *if we accept a mixed-effects fit with shrinkage priors*. Degrees of freedom: 19 probes × 3 conditions × 4 judge reps × 2 judges = 456 observations per clean arm. Residual df for σ_ε in a crossed ANOVA (their Appendix D, Eq. 20): `(nm−1)(K−1) = 56 × 3 = 168`. That's not underpowered for σ_ε — which is why our `σ_ε^cell = 0.1836` replicates across judge families to 3 decimal places. For σ_α: with 19 scenarios the method-of-moments estimator in their Eq. 21 gives a noisy point estimate but a *usable* one — compare to Choi 2026 (2602.00521) IRT estimates, which stabilize around n=30. We're within spitting distance. The move is: **fit the mixed model with a weakly-informative prior on σ_α** (half-Cauchy(0,1), standard), report posterior intervals, and accept that the σ_α interval is wide. That's not unidentified — that's appropriately uncertain. McDonald's 1999 recommendation of ≥5×items/dimension is for factor-analytic *dimension recovery*, not variance-component estimation.

### RIGOR

The degrees-of-freedom argument is the exact place PARSIMONY loses. Walk it: CyclicJudge's crossed ANOVA estimates 4 variance components with (effectively) 4 mean-squares. At n=19, m=1 (we have ONE agent rep per cell because gpt-5.4-mini contaminated the gpt-5.4 column — MATH_ONE_PAGER §2 confirms this), β is **literally non-identifiable from ε**. The `Var(X̄) = σ_α²/n + σ_β²/(nm) + ...` decomposition PARSIMONY cited requires m ≥ 2 to separate β from ε — CyclicJudge Eq. 21 makes this explicit: `E[MS_G] = σ_ε² + Kσ_β²`, which has two unknowns and collapses to one equation at m=1. No prior rescues a structural non-identifiability; a half-Cauchy(0,1) on σ_β doesn't estimate σ_β, it just *pushes the posterior toward the prior* — which PARSIMONY will then quote as "the estimate." That's not appropriate uncertainty; that's circular. Further: for σ_α, Brennan 2001 (the *primary* textbook PARSIMONY gestures at via McDonald) gives the G-coefficient standard-error formula. Plug n=19, K=4, assume σ_α² ≈ σ_ε² (generous): the 95% CI on σ_α² spans roughly `[0.3·point, 3.3·point]` — a *tenfold* uncertainty band. Feuer 2025 §4 doesn't even *try* factor analysis below n=100 because Cronbach's α is biased downward at small n (their α_i^norm is defined on n=500 items). PARSIMONY's "within spitting distance of Choi 30" is wrong by direction: 19 is below, and the bias at 19 is ~10–15% on σ_α² relative to asymptote.

## Round 3 — Probe-family coverage

### PARSIMONY

Probe-family coverage is where the n=1 frame actually cuts the opposite way from what RIGOR assumes. The project has *five* named families (recall, continuity, reconstruction, calibration, efficiency) but only **two** are in the current rubric — factual_grounding and continuity each get meaningful probe coverage; calibration gets 3 sub-categories; efficiency is tracked by the runner and deliberately excluded from the rubric (MATH_ONE_PAGER §8). So "19 probes ÷ 5 families = 3.8/family" is a strawman; the *headline* claim is recall+continuity, which is where 14–16 of the 19 probes live. On recall alone, that's plausibly adequate — Yeadon 2026 (2603.14732) shows criterion-referenceability tracks with task structure, not item count; their physics-grading validity claim lands at n=20–40 items when the referenceability is high. Our R² = 0.754 / 0.862 on the 12-subcategory rubric (WHERE_WE_ARE Apr 21 packet) is *high schematic adherence*, which by Feuer's definition (2509.20293 Eq. 3) means "verdicts derive from factor scores." That R² is the thing RIGOR should want to see, and it's already clearing the Feuer bar on our n=19. For *per-family* claims: agreed, we don't have those yet, but the project isn't making them. The headline is about reconstruction-for-continuation — one construct.

### RIGOR

"We only make one construct claim" is retreat-by-redefinition. The project's own memo (MATH_ONE_PAGER §8) lists three axes: accuracy, continuity, efficiency — and says continuity "has a fuzzy axis and no scoring function" and efficiency has "no composite." That's not one construct, that's one *measured* construct and two promised-but-not-delivered ones. Bean 2025 (2511.04703) §Fig. 2 surveyed 445 benchmarks and found 47.8% had *contested* phenomenon definitions — exactly this failure mode. Their recommendation #2 is "measure the phenomenon directly"; the Syke rubric has ONE subcategory (`state_transition_tracking`) carrying R² = 0.606 / 0.731 *alone* — meaning 11 of the 12 subcategories are largely redundant skins on one latent. That's Feuer 2025's factor-collapse signature (>0.93 cross-loading). Run the numbers: to make a per-family claim at McDonald's ≥5×items rule (which applies to *any* reflective construct, not only dimension-recovery — see McDonald 1999 Ch. 6), continuity alone needs ≥15 dedicated probes; calibration needs ≥15; efficiency needs ≥15. That's ≥45 probes before you even fund recall. PARSIMONY's "high R² clears the Feuer bar" is exactly backward: Feuer's Fig. 1 shows the DANGER signature is when one factor explains everything — which is what our 0.73-alone-R² shows. **Our high R² is the pathology, not the pass.**

## Round 4 — Time / cycle replication

### PARSIMONY

Time replication is the place where I concede the most ground — but not to RIGOR's framing. One 15-day window gives you zero estimate of across-window variance. However: the project's question is *memory-system performance on this user's traces*, not "universal memory benchmark." For an n=1 case study, two windows is the minimum and four is luxurious. Gelman's "you need enough to detect your effect" applies here: if cross-window variance is ~σ_ε-sized (plausible given the Apr 17–21 intra-window stability — `σ_ε^cell` replicated to ±0.0037 across judge families in 5 days), then 2 windows gives you a t-test with 1 df — useless — but 3 windows with 2 df and paired design gets you a usable interval. Four windows × 19 probes × 4 reps is the sweet spot: you estimate `σ_τ` (across-window variance) at 3 df, you keep probe set stable for paired analysis, and wall-clock cost is 4 × 15 days = ~2 months. CyclicJudge (2603.01865 §4.2) didn't do time replication at all — they ran one cycle. LifeBench (2603.03781) is across long real timescales but collapses it into one observation per task. Two windows *is* a meaningful upgrade from one. Twelve would be overkill and would eat a year of human attention for insight we can buy at 4.

### RIGOR

Four windows doesn't buy what PARSIMONY claims it buys. The Syke packet is **one user's real filesystem**, which means window-to-window variation is confounded with (a) the user's actual life events in that window, (b) the memory system's learning curve on this user, and (c) the calendar (a window containing a major project deadline is not exchangeable with one containing a vacation). Bean 2025's §4 explicitly flags convenience sampling: 12.3% of benchmarks sampled only from readily-accessible data, and the authors call this a validity threat. The Syke windows are the extreme case — n=1 user, non-exchangeable windows. Four windows is 4 highly-correlated observations, not 4 iid draws; the effective-n on σ_τ is closer to 1.5. For the claim "this memory system generalizes across time," you need either (i) ≥12 windows to average out calendar/life-event structure (LifeBench-scale), or (ii) an explicit stratified design where you *select* windows to span life-event types and report per-stratum. (i) costs a year; (ii) requires admitting the n=1 user is itself a construct-validity ceiling. MemoryArena 2602.16313 collapses on interdependent-session tasks — their result is that LoCoMo winners lose 30%+ on interdependent tasks, which *means* time-structure matters. Running 4 Syke windows tells you about 4 weeks of one user's life; that's an ethnographic observation, not a generalization claim. Honest move: downgrade the language from "generalizes across time" to "was stable over the Apr 17–May 30 period on this user" and stop.

## Round 5 — Convergence + preserved disagreement

### PARSIMONY

I move on three things and hold on one. **Converged:** (1) σ_β is non-identifiable at m=1 — RIGOR is right, the gpt-5.4-mini contamination means the current packet literally cannot separate agent stochasticity from judge flicker; we need ≥2 clean agent reps per cell, which means one rerun of the 57 cells, not a probe expansion. (2) Per-family claims at current n are not defensible — if the project wants a "continuity" or "calibration" score with its own CI, the per-family probe count has to come up to the McDonald/Brennan ≥15-per-construct zone. (3) Time replication doesn't buy iid draws on n=1 — I now agree 4 windows is better framed as "4 strata of this user's life" than as "4 iid samples," and the publication language should reflect that. **Held:** σ_α is estimable with a prior. Not *precisely* — no one claimed precisely — but a half-Cauchy posterior on σ_α with n=19 is a *real* confidence statement, not a prior echo, because the likelihood shape on 152 clean cells is informative enough to update the prior meaningfully. RIGOR's "you're quoting the prior" critique is a fair warning for *uncritical* reporting but not a veto on the method. Rao 2026 Autorubric's framework itself allows `CANNOT_ASSESS` verdicts and few-shot calibration at sub-100 item counts — the machinery does work at this scale when calibrated.

### RIGOR

I also move, but less. **Converged:** (1) For the *construct-diagnostic* claim the project is actually best positioned to ship ("here is the σ_ε / σ_γ / schematic-adherence diagnostic on a real n=1 packet"), n=19 × 4 reps is adequate — the measurement IS the instrument, and we've measured it. That's a real contribution and it doesn't need more n. (2) A binary useful-vs-fail collapse (κ ≈ 0.86 on our packet, σ_ε^collapsed ≈ 0.029) IS usable at n=19 for a *directional* ordering statement between architectures — the collapse removes most of the identification problem because binary verdicts don't need σ_α cleanly estimated, just a large effect. **Held, firmly:** σ_α posterior-via-prior at n=19 is a mathematical fiction for *architecture-ranking* purposes. The prior determines the tails. PARSIMONY's "the likelihood is informative enough" is asserted, not shown; on a half-Cauchy(0,1) with 19 items, posterior SD of σ_α is dominated by the prior scale for realistic effect sizes — this is standard in hierarchical Bayes (see Gelman's own folk-theorem). We disagree on whether that's *usable*. I say: for a headline claim "architecture A > architecture B on memory reconstruction," no. For a diagnostic claim "our instrument has this flicker profile," yes. The residual disagreement is not about math — it is about what the project is allowed to claim next.

### Moderator synthesis

The two experts converged on *four* things: (a) the current packet cannot separate σ_β from σ_ε without rerunning ≥2 clean agent reps per cell (cheap fix, same n); (b) per-family per-axis claims (continuity-alone, calibration-alone, efficiency-alone) require probe-per-family ≥ 15, implying a total ≥ 60–75 for a multi-axis leaderboard; (c) cross-window "time generalization" at n=1 is not iid and should be reported as stratified observation of one user's life, not as a generalization claim; (d) a binary useful-vs-fail metric is more robust at small-n than the 3-level ordinal for architecture comparison. They still disagree on σ_α-at-n=19. PARSIMONY says prior-regularized inference yields a usable interval; RIGOR says prior-dominance makes it unusable for architecture ranking. This disagreement is *not* resolvable by more thinking — it is a real methodological fork. The decision depends on what claim the project ships. The useful move: be explicit about which claim you're making, and map each claim to the minimum n it requires.

## Decision tree — how much data needed for which claim

| Claim being made | Minimum n (probes × conditions × reps) | Why this number | What it DOESN'T buy you |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Instrument diagnostic** ("judge-flicker σ_ε^cell on this rubric is ~0.18; cross-judge shift is ~+0.57; schematic adherence R² is ~0.80") | **19 × 3 × 4** (current) — ~228 verdicts per judge family | σ_ε residual df at this n is 168; replicates to ±0.004 across judge families. CyclicJudge Eq. 20 applies cleanly. | Architecture ranking. σ_α or σ_β. Per-family claims. Time generalization. |
| **Binary directional comparison** ("arch A produces more useful-vs-fail verdicts than arch B on this user's packet") | **19 × 3 × 2 clean agent reps × 2 judges** (228 observations, agent-rep-clean) | Binary collapse makes κ ≈ 0.86 and σ_ε^collapsed ≈ 0.029 → paired McNemar at d ≈ 3 is powered at n=19. Does NOT require σ_α estimation; uses within-probe paired design. Requires rerun to fix β-contamination. | Ordinal fine-grained ranking. Per-family claims. Σ_α interval. Generalization to other users. |
| **Ordinal architecture ranking, headline** ("arch A > arch B on the 3-level verdict, with CI") | **≥ 50 × 3 × 2 × 2** (600 observations) | Needs σ_α identifiable without prior dominance (Brennan 2001 rule of thumb; CyclicJudge's MT-Bench used n=80 and reported FPC-corrected σ_γ). At n≥50 the half-Cauchy(0,1) prior contributes <20% to posterior σ_α² width at plausible effect sizes. | Per-family claims. Time generalization. Construct validity beyond this rubric. |
| **Per-axis per-family claim** (e.g., "continuity score differs between A and B") | **≥ 15 probes per family × 3 conditions × 2 reps × 2 judges**; for 4 live families = **60 probes minimum** | McDonald 1999 Ch. 6 ≥5×items/construct for reflective constructs at α≥0.7; our current factor-collapse signature (R² = 0.606 on one subcategory alone) means the current rubric cannot support per-family claims at any n until the rubric itself is pruned/rebuilt (Autorubric 2603.00077 per-criterion atomic design). | Ordinal fine-grained between-family interactions. Time generalization. |
| **Time / cycle generalization** ("this memory system is stable across N-week windows") | **≥ 4 windows × 19 probes × 3 conditions × 2 reps** as *stratified observation* (≈1800 observations, n=1 user), OR **≥ 12 windows** for across-window variance as iid | At n=1 user, windows are non-exchangeable; 4 windows is stratified descriptive, not inferential. 12 is the LifeBench-scale requirement to treat windows as iid draws. | Generalization across users. Calendar/life-event interactions unless stratified by design. |
| **Cross-user generalization** ("memory system X is better than Y in general") | **Out of scope for n=1 benchmark** — requires ≥10 users × 19 probes × 3 conditions × 2 reps | n=1 design deliberately excludes this claim (MATH_ONE_PAGER §8). Bean 2025 recommendation #1: define the phenomenon; n=1 defines "this user's phenomenon." | Any population-level claim. |

**Decision-tree shorthand.** If the project ships a construct-diagnostic paper (current path), n=19 × 3 × 4 is enough and the next move is **rerun β-contamination cells, not add probes**. If the project ships architecture rankings, the minimum is **≥ 50 probes × 2 clean agent reps + fix the rubric first**. If per-axis claims are desired, the minimum is **≥ 60 probes with ≥ 15/family + pruned rubric**. If time generalization is desired, **≥ 4 windows framed as stratified observation, or ≥ 12 for inferential**. Cross-user generalization is out of scope and should be retired from the language entirely.

**Residual disagreement preserved.** PARSIMONY: σ_α at n=19 is estimable with a weakly-informative prior and yields a usable — if wide — interval, sufficient for hedged architecture claims. RIGOR: σ_α at n=19 is prior-dominated for architecture ranking, and any headline claim that leans on it is reporting the prior. Both agree this does not block the instrument-diagnostic claim; both agree it blocks the ranking claim — they differ on whether hedged ranking is honest or dishonest at this n. The project should pick a lane and be explicit about which.
