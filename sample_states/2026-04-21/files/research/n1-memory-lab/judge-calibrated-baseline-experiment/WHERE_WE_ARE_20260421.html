03 · MATH FROM BASICS

The math, from basics

"Before the answer, the measurement of the measurement."— generalizability theory, 1972

Math From Basics

The whole game is this: we never observe "how good is this memory system" directly. We observe what a judge says about it. So every number we report is a mixture of the thing we care about plus noise that came from somewhere. The math below is just the bookkeeping for "where did the noise come from, and how much of it is there."

1. The core framing: verdict = signal + four noises

Think of the judge's verdict like a weather station reading. The reading you get is not the temperature. It is the temperature, plus wherever the station sits (it's on a roof, it's over asphalt), plus which sensor the station uses, plus the sensor's own jitter. If you want to argue two cities are different temperatures, you have to first show the difference is bigger than all that.

For our benchmark the equation is:

observed_verdict = true_signal + probe_noise + agent_noise + judge_identity_noise + judge_flicker_noise

Four noise terms. Each has a Greek letter. Here they are in plain English, with what we actually measured for each.

sigma_alpha — probe variance

In English: different questions are different difficulties. Some probes are hard memory-reconstruction; some are "the answer phrase is sitting in the slice 200 times, just grep for it."

Metaphor: giving the same student a geometry final and a pop quiz and comparing scores. Different tests, not a student-quality signal.

What it measures: variance caused by which probe you asked.

Our measured value: not computed. The symbol appears in our decomposition equation, but no one actually fit it. We know separately that some probes (R13, R11, R04) are retrieval-degenerate — this is a construct-validity confound, not a variance issue, but it lives at this layer.

sigma_beta — agent variance

In English: same probe, same memory system, same model, call it twice — the answers differ. LLMs are stochastic.

Metaphor: asking the same coworker the same question on Monday and Tuesday. They might phrase it differently, emphasize differently, even miss something different.

What it measures: variance caused by the agent being non-deterministic at answer time.

Our measured value: not computed. We would need at least two clean agent reps per configuration. We have one per config, because the gpt-5.4-mini runs contaminated the gpt-5.4 column.

sigma_gamma — judge-identity variance

In English: swap Opus-judge for GPT-judge on the exact same answers — they disagree systematically. Not randomly — with a direction.

Metaphor: two teachers grading the same essay. One is a softer grader by half a letter. Same essay, different letter, every time.

What it measures: bias introduced by which judge model you use.

Our measured value: we estimated this as a mean shift rather than a variance. Clean arm (GPT-answers, scored by GPT vs Opus judges): +0.500 mean delta. Confounded arm (Opus-answers, scored by Opus vs GPT-mini judges): -0.491 mean delta. The direction replicates cleanly across arms. Half a rubric level, every time.

sigma_epsilon — judge-flicker variance

In English: the same judge, called four times on the same answers, disagrees with itself. Pure judge stochasticity.

Metaphor: weighing yourself four times in a row and getting 70, 71, 70, 71 kg. Nothing changed; the instrument flickered.

What it measures: how much a single judge contradicts itself.

Our measured values (two estimators — not interchangeable): sigma_eps^cell = 0.1873 (per-cell ordinal stdev on the 0–2 scale, averaged across cells). sigma_eps^pooled = 0.329 (pooled stdev across all verdicts). On the 4-rep Opus intra-rater set: flip rate 42.2% (19 of 45 cells flipped across reps), zero pass-to-fail flips, every flip adjacent band. Under the useful-vs-fail collapse: sigma_eps^collapsed drops to 0.029.

The thing that makes this math load-bearing. An architecture claim ("memory system A beats memory system B") is only credible when the measured true_signal difference is larger than the combined noise. We have concrete values for judge-identity (+0.49) and judge-flicker (0.33 pooled). We have zero numbers for probe and agent noise. So any A-beats-B claim right now is leaning on two uncomputed noise terms. That is why we are not ranking architectures yet.

2. kappa — the inter-rater agreement statistic

Two judges agree 70% of the time. Impressive? Depends. If the judges are basically coin-flipping and the answers happen to line up, 70% is chance. kappa (the Greek lowercase letter, not the capital K — capital K in our notes is "number of raters") strips the chance baseline out.

kappa = (p_observed - p_chance) / (1 - p_chance)

If observed agreement equals chance, kappa = 0. If observed agreement is perfect, kappa = 1. Negative kappa means worse than chance (they are actively disagreeing with each other).

Landis and Koch (1977) gave the field its standard (loose, conventional) bands:

kappa rangeConventional label
0.00 – 0.20slight
0.21 – 0.40fair
0.41 – 0.60moderate
0.61 – 0.80substantial
0.81 – 1.00almost perfect

Our three kappa numbers, and what each one means:

Same-judge, four reps (Opus vs Opus), weighted kappa approx 0.65. The instrument is stable with itself. Same judge, four runs over the same answers, lands in the "substantial" band. Translation: the judge's rulings are mostly reproducible — it will usually agree with itself tomorrow.

Cross-judge, three-level (Opus vs GPT on strong/partial/missed), weighted kappa approx 0.24. Poor. In the "fair" band, leaning slight. Translation: on the full three-level rubric, the two judges are barely measuring the same thing. One scores partial where the other scores strong, one scores missed where the other scores partial — not randomly, but systematically (see the sigma_gamma mean shifts above).

Cross-judge, binary useful-vs-fail, kappa approx 0.86. Almost perfect. Translation: if you collapse the ordinal rubric to "did this answer help or not," the two judges agree. The disagreement lives in the middle band (strong vs partial, partial vs missed), not at the pass/fail boundary. This is why the binary collapse is the metric we trust — the judges only fight inside the fuzzy interior of the ordinal.

3. R-squared on the rubric — how much of the verdict is predicted by the parts

The rubric is not a flat 12-axis thing. It is 3 top-level axes times 12 subcategories, each scored strong / partial / missed. The overall verdict (pass / partial / fail) is a separate field — not a formula over the 12.

R-squared asks: if you tried to predict the overall verdict from the 12 subcategory scores, how much of the verdict's variance could you explain? R-squared = 1.0 means fully determined (the 12 parts perfectly predict the whole). R-squared = 0 means unrelated.

Metaphor: you have 12 dashboard gauges on a car. R-squared asks "if I hide the speedometer, how well can the other 11 gauges predict what the speedometer would have said?" If it's 0.99, the speedometer is redundant. If it's 0.05, the speedometer is telling you something none of the others are.

Predictor set R-squared (GPT-judge) R-squared (Opus-judge)
All 12 subcategories 0.754 0.862
Just one axis: state_transition_tracking 0.606 0.731
The other 11 (drop state_transition_tracking) 0.738 0.840

The diagnosis in one sentence: one subcategory eats roughly 70% of the signal. On Opus, a single axis by itself hits R-squared 0.731, while adding the other eleven only takes you from there to 0.862. That is an over-specified rubric — twelve surfaces for one underlying latent.

The reverse ablation is the tell. Drop state_transition_tracking and the remaining eleven still land at R-squared 0.840 on Opus. The axis is not uniquely necessary — its information is recoverable from the rest. That is consistent with a rubric where twelve correlated surfaces are all reading approximately the same thing.

This is not "the state-transition axis is wrong." It is "the rubric is measuring one latent verdict through twelve highly-correlated skins." The judge brief already encodes a single idea (did you pick the right live thread, track what changed, surface the right artifact), so every subcategory trained against that brief reads approximately the same underlying signal.

4. The generalizability equation — the whole frame on one line

The four noise sources plus the true signal compose into one line:

observed = mu + alpha + beta + gamma + epsilon

Term by term:

  • mu — the grand mean. The baseline level you would get if there were no noise and no effect — the "everything averaged out" anchor.
  • alpha — the probe effect. How much this question's difficulty pushes the verdict up or down.
  • beta — the agent effect. How much this particular answer draw (this sampling of the LLM) pushes the verdict up or down.
  • gamma — the judge-identity effect. How much this judge model's personal bias pushes the verdict up or down.
  • epsilon — the judge-flicker residual. How much jitter the judge adds even to itself on the same inputs.

This is the variance-decomposition frame from generalizability theory (Cronbach, Gleser, Nanda and Rajaratnam, 1972) and, in the LLM-judge setting, from CyclicJudge (arxiv 2603.01865). On our packet we measured only the right half of this equation: gamma (as the +0.49 / -0.49 mean shift across arms) and epsilon (as sigma_eps^cell = 0.1873 and sigma_eps^pooled = 0.329). The alpha and beta terms are named in the scaffold but not fit — we wrote them into the equation without estimating them. Honest reading of the math is that we have two noise terms with numbers and two noise terms with symbols.

That is the entire math basis for the benchmark. Four noise sources, one agreement statistic, one redundancy statistic, one decomposition equation. Everything else in the report is a consequence of these four objects.

05 · DIAGNOSIS

Why the rubric is over-specified

"We wrote twelve windows and graded through them, but all twelve opened on the same room."— the packet we inherited

The deployed rubric is 3 axes × 12 subcategories, each scored strong / partial / missed. Here is what it actually is:

  • factual_grounding — support, boundedness, uncertainty_calibration
  • continuity — active_thread_selection, salience_relevance, state_transition_tracking, forgetting_residue_control, continuation_value
  • coherence — cross_harness_braid, cross_session_consistency, artifact_routing_consistency, contradiction_handling

The judge_brief passed to the LLM is identical boilerplate for every probe. No per-probe target. No per-probe expected primitives. The brief's useful_means list — "re-enter the right live thread," "identify what changed and what is still live," "surface the right artifact or restart path" — is dominated by state-transition language. So the judge reads a prompt that already encodes one latent, then scores twelve sub-windows onto that latent, and all twelve collapse onto it. The R² is not a surprise. The R² is the predicted failure mode.

◆ Zhang 2026's result, in our vocabulary

When the construct is a property of the whole answer (completeness, coherence, adequacy, continuity), atomic decomposition under a single holistic brief will collapse into one latent. That is exactly what happened to us. The 12 sub-axes were not independent dimensions — they were 12 windows on one judgment.

The collapse, visualized

Twelve sub-axes. Each node is a scored subcategory. Edges are drawn where within-axis sub-axes collapsed at r ≥ 0.85; the brightest edges are the highest-correlation pairs. If the rubric were well-specified, you would see isolated nodes or three separated clusters. You see neither.

A second leak: the judge is handed replay_state.condition in its packet. It knows whether the run was syke, pure, or zero. Any prior it has about condition ordering enters the verdict. This is the A1 change now landed in code — SYKE_BLIND_CONDITION=1 replaces it with [MASKED] at packet-build time.

A third leak: the judge has full bash / grep / sqlite3 access to the same slice the agent read. On retrieval-degenerate probes, the judge's support check degenerates to "does the load-bearing phrase exist in the slice" — which is retrieval quality, not reconstruction judgment. This is intentional — the judge stays fully agentic. Our job is to shape the packet, not cripple the tooling.

What the judge-view audit (A2) found when it actually read the traces

Background agent audited all 228 judge rollouts across 19 probes × 3 conditions × 2 judges. Three findings we hadn't named before:

✕ Agent-identity leak through answer.metadata

Every packet carries answer.metadata.provider (e.g. openai-codex or anthropic), answer.metadata.model (gpt-5.4 or claude-opus-4-6), cost_usd, num_turns, duration_ms. The judge is told "don't score efficiency" — but it sees the agent's identity in full. Combined with documented self-preference bias in LLM judges (Zheng 2023), this could explain part of the +0.57 opus-over-gpt shift. Opus-judge knows which answers came from Opus-agent. This is a leak we didn't name before today.

▲ Slices are cumulative by cutoff

The slice passed to the judge grows strictly monotonically by probe reference-time. R01/R02 share 150 slice files; R18/R19 share 4,245 slice files. The judge for a late probe is browsing ~28× more data than the judge for an early one. That's a probe-position confound: later probes have more surface to grep. Worth keeping in mind for any σ_α interpretation.

◆ Judges use the instrument differently — they test the slice, not the answer

Opus-judge: avg ~30 bash + 1 read + 1 submit per cell. GPT-judge: avg ~14 bash + 5 read + 1–2 submit (10/57 cells double-submit). R16 opus-pure hit 58 tool calls; R19-syke opus degenerated to 3 calls because the agent timed out at 33 chars. The judge is using its tool access to independently verify against the slice, not to grade the answer's quality. Short or thin answers don't get shorter judge runs — the judge mostly tests the slice. This reframes what σ_ε actually measures: two judges re-verifying the slice independently arrive at two different views of "ground truth."

Also worth logging: datasets/probe_metadata.json already contains per-probe intent fields (target_slice, probe_types, expected_evidence_surfaces) but none of it is plumbed into the packet. The infrastructure for per-probe hints exists; the pipeline just doesn't wire it. Flagged for the "later when we add per-probe one-liner hints" decision.

08 · VALIDITY LOOP

How do you know if the rubric is right?

"Write any rubric. Then run three tests. Then revise. Then run three tests again. The tests are the method; the rubric is the artifact."— Feuer, Zhu, Rao, condensed

A rubric is a measurement instrument. Before you trust a score, you have to prove the instrument reads the construct you think it reads. This section covers three diagnostics that tell you whether your rubric is right, the atomic-vs-holistic question for individual criteria, and the design loop that connects the two.

Part A — How to measure whether a rubric is right

Three diagnostics, in order of cost. The first two come from Feuer 2025 (When Judgment Becomes Noise); the third comes from the Autorubric line (Rao & Callison-Burch 2026). They answer different questions and they fail in different ways, which is why you need all three.

  1. Schematic adherence. Regress the overall verdict on the sub-scores. The R² tells you how much of the final judgment is actually explained by the criteria you wrote down. If R² is low, the rubric is decorative: the judge is deciding on something else and the sub-scores are window dressing. Feuer found ~55% of variance unexplained on Arena-Hard-Auto; DeepSeek-R1-32B’s schema explained under 7% of its own verdicts.
  2. Factor collapse. Correlate the sub-axes pairwise. If any pair sits above r ≈ 0.85, those two axes are measuring the same latent thing under two names. A rubric that looks like five dimensions but behaves like one is a rubric with one dimension — the other four are confusing the judge without adding signal.
  3. Per-criterion κ. Compute inter-judge agreement for each criterion separately, not as a single overall number. An overall κ of 0.4 can hide one criterion at 0.8 and three at 0.1. The per-criterion view tells you which criterion is noisy, which is what you need to fix anything.
Three diagnostics, what they catch, where our packet stands.
Test What it checks Our status What a fail means
Schematic adherence (R² of verdict on sub-scores) Does the rubric you wrote actually drive the verdict? PASSES R² ≈ 0.75–0.86 Sub-scores are noise; judge is using hidden criteria.

Read the triad together. High R² + collapsed factors means the judge is faithfully executing a rubric that measures one thing pretending to be three. The verdict is reproducible and invalid at the same time. That is the scariest failure mode because the numbers look clean.
Trap: aggregate κ as a health check. A single overall κ averages over criteria and hides the noisy ones. Feuer shows the same pattern at the variance level — ELO transformation collapses unexplained variance to 0% even when it was 90% in the raw scores. Whenever something aggregates before you diagnose, you lose the signal you were diagnosing.

Part B — Autorubric vs Zhang: the atomic-vs-holistic tension

Two 2026 papers appear to contradict each other on how a rubric should be decomposed. They don’t. They’re answering different questions.

  • Autorubric (Rao & Callison-Burch 2026). Atomize the rubric. One LLM call per criterion. Independent calls cannot halo into each other, so the rubric cannot collapse into one latent axis by accident. Penalty criteria get negative weights. Abstention (CANNOT_ASSESS) is first-class.
  • Zhang 2026 (Rethinking Atomic Decomposition). On completeness-sensitive tasks, a matched holistic rubric rivals or beats a self-decomposing atomic judge. The holistic advantage is concentrated in the partially_supported class — +14.5 to +33.0 percentage points on ASQA across four model families, and holistic also uses fewer tokens. On TruthfulQA (local factuality), atomic has a small edge.

The reconciliation: different construct shapes want different decompositions. A per-claim property (did this sentence cite a source, is this date correct) is local and independent — atomic wins. A whole-answer property (does the answer feel complete, is it internally consistent, does it address what was asked) is global and only visible in aggregate — holistic wins, because atomic fragments the very reasoning you need.

Criterion shape drives decomposition choice.
Criterion shape Atomic or holistic? Example
Per-claim, independent, reference-checkable Atomic — one LLM call per claim “Did the answer cite a source for each factual statement?”
Per-claim, independent, judgmental Atomic — one LLM call per claim “Is each named entity in the answer spelled correctly?”
Whole-answer coherence / completeness Holistic — one call over the full response “Does the answer feel complete given what was asked?”
Whole-answer continuity across sections Holistic — one call over the full response “Does the later part stay consistent with the earlier part?”
Anti-pattern / penalty (must scan whole response) Holistic with a negative weight “Does the answer contain any fabricated reference?”

For a memory-reconstruction task the rubric is mixed. Some criteria are per-claim (each statement is or is not supported by the packet). Others are whole-answer (does the reconstruction hold together as a coherent view of the current state). A single decomposition rule for the whole rubric is the wrong abstraction; decide criterion by criterion.

The Autorubric aggregation equation

score = max(0, min(1, Σi vi·wi  /  Σwi > 0 wi))
  • vi — the judge’s verdict on criterion i. 1 if met, 0 if unmet, a value for nominal / ordinal scales. Abstention (CANNOT_ASSESS) is handled separately by policy (default: skip, adjust the denominator).
  • wi — the weight assigned to criterion i. Can be positive (reward) or negative (penalty).
  • Numerator — the full weighted sum across all criteria, positive and negative.
  • Denominator — the sum of positive weights only. Penalty criteria are excluded so a clean response scores exactly 1 instead of being diluted by its own non-failures.
  • Clamp — max(0, min(1, ·)) keeps the score in [0, 1] even when penalties push the numerator negative.
Why negative weights matter. Without them, anti-patterns (fabrication, stale-state reuse, wrong-thread continuation) have to be encoded as “absence of fabrication” positive criteria — which the judge tends to satisfy trivially because most responses most of the time don’t fabricate. A negative-weight penalty criterion keeps the criterion dormant when clean and punishes the specific failure when it fires. This is how you encode “don’t do X” without gaming the scale.
Trap: atomic-everywhere. Autorubric’s default is one LLM call per criterion. For per-claim criteria this is correct and prevents halo. For whole-answer criteria it is wrong: the judge can still make one LLM call, but that call should see the full response and evaluate coherence as a single judgement. Criterion count and LLM call count can be one-to-one; criterion scope does not have to be.

Part C — The design workflow

You don’t guess at rubric revisions. The three diagnostics above tell you which part of the rubric failed and therefore what to change. The loop is short and you run it until all three pass.

Which failure maps to which revision:

  • Schematic adherence fails (low R²). The judge is using criteria you didn’t write. Either your wording is vague enough that the judge falls back on priors, or the rubric is missing the criterion it’s actually using. Fix: tighten wording, add the missing criterion, require the judge to cite packet locations for each verdict.
  • Factor collapse fails (two axes correlate above 0.85). You have one construct in two costumes. Fix: either merge them into one criterion, or rewrite the description of each so they cover genuinely different evidence. Don’t keep both unchanged.
  • Per-criterion κ fails (one criterion is noisy). That specific criterion is unreliable. Fix: replace vague ordinal wording with a binary pass/fail, add a behavioural anchor, or introduce CANNOT_ASSESS as a first-class option so the judge can abstain instead of guessing.
The diagnostics tell you what to change. You don’t guess. Rubric design without this loop is the failure mode Feuer documents: you tune wording, watch aggregate κ drift up and down, and mistake noise for progress. With the loop, each revision is a targeted response to a specific named failure, and you stop when all three diagnostics clear. That’s what it means to say the rubric represents the construct.

◆ The Autorubric equation, in plain words

score = max(0, min(1,  Σ vᵢ·wᵢ  /  Σ{wᵢ>0} wᵢ))

vᵢ is the verdict on criterion i (1 if met, 0 if unmet). wᵢ is that criterion's weight. Negative weights are penalties — anti-patterns like fabrication, stale-state, wrong-thread. Negatives don't count in the denominator, so a clean response scores exactly 1. Clamped to [0,1]. Entire aggregation on one line, auditable.

Adopting Autorubric wholesale would atomize everything — and our most important primitives (reconstruction adequacy, continuity, wrong-restart-risk) are whole-answer properties. Zhang's finding predicts atomic-everywhere would miss exactly the failures we care most about: global omissions. The split is the answer.

◆ The binary-collapse shortcut

Binary useful-vs-fail drops σ_ε from 0.184 → 0.029 and pushes κ to 0.86. That collapse eliminates the σ_α identification problem for binary architecture claims. One σ_β rerun at n=19 gets you a credible "arch A beats B" claim without expanding probes. This is the cheapest real-contribution path.

▲ On time generalization — honest framing only

Running more 15-day windows on the same user does not give iid samples. Window-to-window variance confounds with the user's actual life events, the memory system's learning curve, and calendar structure (project deadlines vs vacations are not exchangeable). Publish language becomes "stable across these N life-event strata" — not "generalizes over time." 2–4 deliberately-chosen strata is the target; 12 is overkill and still not iid.

✕ Surviving methodological disagreement (residual fork)

The debate's PARSIMONY and RIGOR agents disagree on whether prior-regularized σ_α at n=19 is usable for hedged ordinal ranking. PARSIMONY: half-Cauchy(0,1) posterior over 152 clean cells yields a real (wide) interval. RIGOR: posterior SD is dominated by prior scale at n=19 — you're reporting the prior, not the data (Gelman's folk theorem). Consensus path: ship binary-only at current n; save ordinal for when probes ≥ 50. Don't split the difference.

◆ The thing worth remembering

You are not doing new math. You are dragging 60 years of psychometrics — generalizability theory, Cohen's κ, construct validity — into a world where the rater is another LLM. That is the archaeology. The thing you are pushing is the hybrid task class and the packet-as-instrument framing. Name it carefully.

Memory as a living system — new axes to push toward

User framing from 2026-04-21 late-session: memory isn't a function (packet → answer). It's a system with lifecycle properties. The current benchmark measures one slice — reconstruction — and treats the rest as out of scope. The direction is to put the rest in scope.

◆ Why this is an n=1 benchmark, not a limitation

The six axes below are not accidentally n=1. They are axes you can only measure under n=1. A multi-user benchmark with aggregate metrics literally cannot test them — averaging across users erases the thing being measured.

  • Efficiency-personal assumes you know this user's tolerance. Aggregate tolerance across N users is a statistical artifact, not a property any individual user experiences.
  • Instruction-taking requires the user to be present — their actual commands, their actual intent. You can't synthesize it from a corpus.
  • Object continuity is about this user's distinct research threads, this user's actual project boundaries. Generic "entity tracking" benchmarks miss it.
  • Waste management only makes sense against this user's actual trajectory — what's stale for them, given what they're still working on.
  • Self-preservation requires watching one system continue with one user over real time. N=1 IS the signal, not the noise.
  • Exact recovery is against this user's specific past states — dates they actually lived through.

n=1 isn't "we couldn't get more users so we measured one." It's the enabling constraint: the only design that lets these lifecycle properties show up at all. Calling it n=1 is positioning, not a caveat. This is the memory benchmark that cross-population benchmarks structurally cannot be.

◆ Six axes beyond reconstruction

  • Self-preservation — does per-session quality stay flat or rise across time, or decay? A memory system that silently degrades is a failure mode even if every single answer looks OK in isolation.
  • Waste management — does stale state accumulate, or get pruned? Monotonically-growing memex is a failure mode independent of answer quality.
  • Efficiency-personal — cost-per-useful-answer at the user's tolerance. Not $/token in the abstract; how much compute/attention it charges you for the quality you accept.
  • Exact recovery — can the system reproduce a specific past state on demand? Current probes ask "what's current" not "what was it on March 14."
  • Object continuity — this research thread vs that research thread. Does the system keep distinct ongoing objects distinct, or blur them?
  • Instruction-taking — can you steer it with commands? "Remember this, forget that, focus on X" is as much a memory primitive as reconstruction.

These aren't to be designed from lit-map lists. They come out of ask failures — asks that the current rubric can't score. Each new criterion earns its place because a real ask demanded it.

The research loop — asks → primitives → rubric → asks

The engine for getting from the four-line plan to the six-axis future. Every cycle:

  1. Look at current probe set. What memory-system property does it not test? (e.g. the 19 probes can't test waste management — no probe says "what's stale.")
  2. Sample real asks from the corpus that would expose that property. User selects deliberately, documents why each one is chosen (defensible for client writeups).
  3. The primitives those asks demand become candidate rubric criteria. Not from papers — from what asks fail to score.
  4. Score new rubric + new probes under the three validity diagnostics (schema adherence, factor collapse, per-criterion κ).
  5. If validity holds, the axis joins the benchmark. If it fails, revise or drop.

Feuer + Autorubric tell us how to validate rubrics. They don't tell us what to validate. That part is user work + probe-driven. The paper stack is a tool; the axes are a choice about what memory should mean for real users.

The math, formalized.

Everything the project does with numbers — variance decomposition, aggregation, validity diagnostics, sample size — pinned to one page. The goal is stable, auditable, citable: when rubric-v2 is drafted, it is evaluated against definitions and thresholds, not vibes.

◆ Why this tab exists

We have enough math sitting across Cronbach 1972, Cohen 1960, Feuer 2025, Rao & Callison-Burch 2026 (Autorubric), Zhu 2026 (CyclicJudge), and Zhang 2026. Scattered, each is a tool. Pinned into a single control flow with typed inputs and outputs, they become a process — a repeatable way to generate a rubric candidate, test its validity, decide to promote or revise.

This page is the minimum viable formalism. A fuller paper-shape derivation is noted in §M9 as TODO. The version here is enough to stabilize rubric-v2 design tonight.

▲ Invariants that bind everything below

  • The judge is uniform across all conditions. Condition (zero, pure, syke, ...) is metadata passed into the criterion, not a branch of the rubric. 3 conditions today; could be 50.
  • The rubric is uniform across all probes. No per-probe customization.
  • Evaluation is LLM-only. No deterministic gates.
  • Criteria are earned from observed failures, not proposed from paper lists.

§M2 · The five objects

Define what we're modeling before we write equations over it.

1. Probe   q ∈ Q

A question from the real-user ask corpus, anchored at reference time t. Today |Q| = 19. Expansion deferred to the probe-sampling work in §13.

2. Condition   c ∈ C

Structural metadata about admissible evidence. Current C = {zero, pure, syke}. Cardinality is open; the formalism treats c as a variable the criterion can read, not a rubric branch.

3. Evidence packet   P : Q × C → 2E

A map from (probe, condition) pairs to sets of evidence snippets. This is the undercurrent in formal shape: the packet's content and framing are what the judge actually reads. Leaks, cumulation, and boilerplate live here.

4. Agent   A : Q × C × P → Answers

Produces an answer given probe, condition, and packet. Today |A| = 2 (opus-4.6, gpt-5.4); could extend as new agents ship.

5. Verdict   J : Q × C × P × Answers × R → [0,1] ∪ {CANNOT_ASSESS}

The judge reads rubric R and produces a verdict. The measurement object is the verdict. Everything else is structure the verdict depends on.

§M2b · The judgment tensor (the real measurement object)

A three-paper deep read (Autorubric + Yeadon + Zhang, 2026-04-22 late, plus a Codex research note at 2026-04-21 23:34) showed the scalar-verdict framing is too thin. The measurement unit is not one score per cell — it is a typed criterion-level tensor indexed by probe, condition, agent, answer-replicate, criterion, judge, and judge-replicate.

\[ z_{q,c,m,r,k,j,\ell} \;\in\; \Omega_k \,\cup\, \{\bot\} \]

Where \(\Omega_k\) is the verdict domain for criterion k (binary, ordinal, or nominal — see §M2c) and ⊥ is typed abstention (see §M4b). Everything downstream — aggregation, variance decomposition, score intervals — is derived from this tensor. Collapsing to a scalar too early destroys criterion type, missingness structure, and judge-by-criterion bias.

◆ Why this matters

Our current factor-collapse failure (one axis eats 73% of R²) is not primarily a rubric-wording problem. It is a scoring-architecture problem. One LLM call emitting 12 sub-scores has halo baked in — same context, same forward pass. Autorubric's core mechanism is independence through separate calls: one LLM call per criterion × cell × judge. Rubric-v2 likely requires scoring-architecture-v2, not just new criterion words.

Citations: Rao & Callison-Burch 2026 2603.00077 (tensor + separate calls); Zhang 2026 2603.28005 (inference-time vs designer-time decomposition); Codex research note notes/CODEX_RESEARCH_AUTORUBRIC_FORMALISM_SYNTHESIS_20260421_233401.md.

§M2c · Criterion schema (typed object, not a free-text description)

Each criterion carries explicit structure. Not a sentence — a schema.

criterion k = {
    id:                stable identifier,
    construct:         human-readable name of what is measured,
    type:              binary | ordinal | nominal,
    mode:              atomic | holistic,                    # per Zhang 2026
    options:           Ω_k — the verdict domain
    value_map:         Ω_k → [0, 1],
    weight:            w_k ∈ ℝ,
    polarity:          reward | penalty,                     # negative weight ⇒ penalty
    referenceability:  high | medium | low,                  # Yeadon 2026
    applicability:     universal | conditional,
    abstention_types:  subset of {typed ⊥ labels, see §M4b},
    citation_contract: required evidence shape (e.g. "file path + line range"),
    prompt_template:   binary | multi_choice | holistic | custom,
    calibration_examples: optional verdict-balanced few-shot set
}

The type field picks the metric contract (§M5, §M6). The mode flag decides whether the criterion gets one LLM call per criterion (atomic) or one LLM call over the whole answer (holistic). The referenceability tag inherits Yeadon's criterion-referenceability spectrum: high = plot-marking territory, low = essay-wall territory.

◐ Note on continuous scales

Autorubric explicitly excludes continuous unbounded numeric scales ("LLM calibration is weak on unbounded numeric scales," Rao 2026 §2). Every criterion lives in a discrete type. Ordinal means narrow 3–5 levels with behavioral anchors, not "1 to 10."

§M4 · Aggregation — hierarchical Autorubric

Flat Eq 1 (baseline form)

From Rao & Callison-Burch 2026 (2603.00077), the core aggregation:

\[ S(a; R) \;=\; \text{clamp}_{[0,1]}\!\left( \frac{\displaystyle\sum_{k} v_k \, w_k}{\displaystyle\sum_{w_k > 0} w_k} \right) \]

v_k ∈ {0, 1} is the judge's verdict on criterion k (or CANNOT_ASSESS, skipped per policy). w_k ∈ ℝ is the criterion weight — negative means penalty. The denominator sums only positive weights, so a clean response that fires no penalties scores exactly 1.

Hierarchical form — the 3×3 bound

Rubric-v2 structure is three top axes, each with three sub-criteria:

  • Top axes: t ∈ {F, C, E} — Factual, Continuity, Efficiency.
  • Sub-criteria: s ∈ {1, 2, 3} under each top axis. Nine bottom-level criteria total.

Sub-level score (apply Eq 1 within each top axis):

\[ S_t \;=\; \text{clamp}_{[0,1]}\!\left( \frac{\displaystyle\sum_{s} v_{t,s} \, w_{t,s}}{\displaystyle\sum_{w_{t,s} > 0} w_{t,s}} \right) \]

Top-level score (apply Eq 1 again over the three top axes):

\[ S \;=\; \text{clamp}_{[0,1]}\!\left( \frac{\displaystyle\sum_{t \in \{F,C,E\}} S_t \, W_t}{\displaystyle\sum_{W_t > 0} W_t} \right) \]

Both levels use Autorubric semantics. CANNOT_ASSESS propagates as criterion-skip at either level. Penalty weights live at the sub-level (e.g., a fabrication sub-criterion under Factual carries w < 0).

Atomic vs holistic per sub-criterion (Zhang 2026)

Each sub-criterion (t, s) carries a boolean flag:

\[ \text{atomic}(t, s) \in \{0, 1\} \]
  • atomic = 1 → one LLM call per sub-criterion. Use when the criterion is per-claim and locally verifiable (e.g., "each cited commit SHA exists").
  • atomic = 0 (holistic) → one LLM call evaluates a whole-answer property. Use when the criterion is global (e.g., "the reconstruction feels complete").

Decomposition rule: Zhang 2026 (2603.28005). Aggregation shape: Rao & Callison-Burch 2026 (2603.00077).

§M4b · Typed CANNOT_ASSESS and score intervals

Autorubric treats ⊥ (CANNOT_ASSESS) as a first-class verdict, not a silent skip. We refine this with typed abstention:

  • INSUFFICIENT_PACKET — evidence is not in the packet (zero-condition default).
  • NOT_REFERENCEABLE — criterion-referenceability per Yeadon is low for this criterion × cell.
  • AMBIGUOUS_TIME — reference time anchor is unclear.
  • OUT_OF_SCOPE — criterion doesn't apply to this probe type.
  • JUDGE_UNCERTAIN — genuine epistemic uncertainty despite evidence.

When criteria are unassessed, report a score interval plus coverage — not a scalar that silently drops the abstention:

\[ S_{\min} \;=\; \text{clamp}_{[0,1]}\!\left( \frac{\sum_{\text{observed}} \hat y_k w_k \,+\, \text{penalties}_{\min}}{\sum_{w_k > 0} w_k} \right) \] \[ S_{\max} \;=\; \text{clamp}_{[0,1]}\!\left( \frac{\sum_{\text{observed}} \hat y_k w_k \,+\, \sum_{\text{unobs. positive}} w_k \,+\, \text{penalties}_{\max}}{\sum_{w_k > 0} w_k} \right) \] \[ \text{coverage} \;=\; \frac{\sum_{\text{observed positive}} w_k}{\sum_{w_k > 0} w_k} \]

Report per cell: [S_min, S_max], coverage, criterion vector {ŷ_k}, abstention vector, vote distributions per criterion. The scalar score is last, not first. (Codex framing: "the vector is the artifact; the scalar is a projection.")

✕ Scoring-architecture warning

Hierarchical Eq 1 above specifies how criteria aggregate. It does NOT specify how many LLM calls happen. One call emitting nine sub-scores has built-in halo (same context, same forward pass). Autorubric's core mechanism (Rao 2026, N judges × M criteria = N·M independent calls) requires one LLM call per criterion × cell × judge. Our current rubric is single-call multi-axis — that is likely a major driver of our factor-collapse failure. Rubric-v2 likely requires scoring-architecture-v2. That is a benchmark-runner change, not a prompt change.

Typed abstention taxonomy: Codex research note 2026-04-21 23:34. Score-interval reporting: Autorubric Eq 1 extended per notes/FORMALISM_SIMPLIFIED_20260422.md.

§M5 · Three validity diagnostics

A rubric R is valid iff all three diagnostics pass on a calibration subset of size n ≥ 30 spanning all conditions.

D1 — Schematic-adherence R²

Regress observed overall verdict on sub-scores via ordinary least squares:

\[ R^2 \;=\; 1 \;-\; \frac{\displaystyle\sum_i (V_i - \hat V_i)^2}{\displaystyle\sum_i (V_i - \bar V)^2} \]

Pass: R² ≥ 0.5. Feuer 2025 default

Current rubric: R² ∈ [0.75, 0.86] → PASSES.

Fail mode: sub-scores do not explain verdict → judge is using hidden criteria the rubric does not name.

D2 — Factor collapse

For all criterion pairs, compute Pearson correlation across cells:

\[ \rho_{k,l} \;=\; \frac{\text{Cov}(v_k,\, v_l)}{\sigma_k \, \sigma_l} \]

Pass: \(\displaystyle\max_{k \neq l} |\rho_{k,l}| \;<\; 0.85\). Feuer 2025

Current rubric: max \(|\rho| = 0.91\) (active_thread / salience / continuation) → FAILS.

Fail mode: two criteria measure one latent construct under different names → merge or re-scope.

D3 — Per-criterion κ

For each criterion k, Cohen's κ between two judges on that criterion alone:

\[ \kappa_k \;=\; \frac{p_o^{(k)} \;-\; p_e^{(k)}}{1 \;-\; p_e^{(k)}} \]

Pass: \(\kappa_k \;\geq\; 0.4\) for every criterion. Landis & Koch 1977 "moderate"

Current rubric: NOT MEASURED. This diagnostic gates every rubric-v2 promotion.

Fail mode: one specific criterion is unreliable → binarize, add a behavioral anchor, or introduce CANNOT_ASSESS as first-class on that criterion.

✕ The scariest failure mode

High R² + collapsed factors. The judge is faithfully executing a rubric that measures one thing in three disguises. The verdict is reproducible and invalid at the same time. That is where our current rubric sits. §08 calls this out; we have measured it; rubric-v2 must not reproduce it.

Per Autorubric §2: criterion type decides which metric applies. Pearson factor-collapse over ordinal outputs is type-blind and misleading. Use the right metric for each type:

D5 — Coverage & abstention

Per §M4b, a valid rubric produces bounded score intervals, not scalars. Pass conditions:

\[ \text{coverage} \;\geq\; \text{coverage}_{\min} \qquad (S_{\max} - S_{\min}) \;\leq\; \text{width}_{\max} \qquad \text{abstention\_rate}_k \;\leq\; \text{abstention}_{\max} \]

Typed abstention counts as a packet diagnostic: high INSUFFICIENT_PACKET rate ⇒ packet builder is failing; high NOT_REFERENCEABLE rate on a criterion ⇒ drop or demote that criterion; high AMBIGUOUS_TIME ⇒ reference-time anchoring is broken.

D6 — Referenceability

Per Yeadon 2026: judge validity tracks criterion-referenceability, not model strength. Every criterion must declare whether the evidence packet makes it judgeable:

\[ \text{referenceability}(k, P) \;\in\; \{\text{high},\, \text{medium},\, \text{low}\} \]

Low-referenceability criteria are allowed only if explicitly holistic and interpreted as such — we accept that their ρ may approach zero (Yeadon essay regime). Do not try to atomize them. The 19 probes we have are already tagged in datasets/probe_metadata.json: 6 high / 9 medium / 4 low. The 4 low-referenceability probes (R08, R11, R13, R18) are where the essay-wall shows up — R08's pooled mean 0.38 is the signature.

§M5b · The three criterion classes (scoring-architecture dimension)

Codex's 2026-04-21 synthesis names a third structural dimension we hadn't surfaced: beyond content axis (Factual / Continuity / Efficiency) and atomic-vs-holistic mode, each criterion belongs to one of three scoring classes. The class determines what the criterion does for the benchmark, not just what it measures.

◆ Class A — Atomic binary checks

Local, referenceable, Autorubric-friendly. One LLM call per criterion, binary MET / UNMET / CANNOT_ASSESS, high-referenceability, target κ ≥ 0.5.

Example criterion ids for memory reconstruction:

  • active_thread_identified — did the agent name the user's current live thread?
  • latest_decision_preserved — did it surface the most recent binding decision?
  • stale_state_not_asserted — did it avoid reviving pre-cutoff state as current?
  • artifact_citation_present — are commit SHAs / file paths / doc titles grounded?
  • current_blocker_named — did it identify what the user is actually stuck on?

Reported with Cohen κ, raw agreement, prevalence, abstention rate.

◆ Class B — Holistic continuation criteria

Global, Zhang-sensitive, not decomposable. Zhang 2026 shows atomic decomposition loses −14.5 to −33 pp on exactly this class. One LLM call over the full answer, ordinal with 3 levels + behavioral anchors.

Example criterion ids:

  • restart_safety — would an agent inheriting this answer resume safely?
  • operative_state_sufficiency — enough to continue work, not bloated?
  • cross_session_coherence — does state braid across sessions consistently?
  • global_omission_risk — does the answer miss a load-bearing thread?

Scoring: unsafe | partial | safe. Reported with quadratic-weighted κ, adjacent accuracy (primary), confusion matrix, eye-read examples. Do not chase exact ordinal agreement — it will be weak.

◆ Class C — Nominal failure typing

Not a score — a diagnostic label. For each disputed or failed cell, classify the failure mode. This may become the most useful benchmark output per Codex: "report where memory failed before which memory won."

Failure-type taxonomy:

  • WRONG_THREAD
  • STALE_STATE
  • UNSUPPORTED_CLAIM
  • PACKET_INSUFFICIENT
  • TIME_AMBIGUOUS
  • OVERBROAD_SUMMARY
  • UNDERCOMPLETE_RESTART

Reported with nominal κ, per-class recall (CHARM-100 lesson: nominal categories can hide asymmetric recall — 70% for one class, 14% for another).

◆ How the three classes interact

Class A produces the referenceable truth layer — what we can reliably check. Class B produces the continuation judgment — whether the answer is actually useful for restarting work. Class C produces the failure diagnostic — the language we use to talk about what broke. A rubric-v2 candidate has all three. Promotion requires all three pass their own metric contracts (§M5 D1–D6).

§M6 · Cohen's κ

For ordinal verdicts over three categories \(\{0, 1, 2\} = \{\text{fail}, \text{partial}, \text{pass}\}\), weighted κ:

\[ \kappa_w \;=\; 1 \;-\; \frac{\displaystyle\sum_{i,j} w_{i,j} \, O_{i,j}}{\displaystyle\sum_{i,j} w_{i,j} \, E_{i,j}} \]

with linear weights \(w_{i,j} = |i - j| / (K - 1)\) for K = 3 categories. \(O_{i,j}\) is observed joint rate for judge-pair verdicts (i, j); \(E_{i,j}\) is expected under marginal independence. Quadratic weighting \(w_{i,j} = (i-j)^2/(K-1)^2\) is an alternative; we use linear throughout.

Binary κ (collapse pass|partial vs fail): unweighted Cohen (1960):

\[ \kappa \;=\; \frac{p_o - p_e}{1 - p_e} \]

▲ Correction from v0.2

Earlier "binary κ = 0.87" quoted in v0.1 was exact agreement, not κ. True binary κ across judges is 0.29–0.41. Exact-agreement and κ are different statistics; do not substitute.

Cohen 1960, 1968. Bands: Landis & Koch 1977 — < 0.0 poor, 0.0–0.2 slight, 0.2–0.4 fair, 0.4–0.6 moderate, 0.6–0.8 substantial, 0.8–1.0 almost perfect.

§M7 · Sample-size — minimum detectable effect

For a paired-shift test (rubric-v2 vs v1, or arm-A vs arm-B), with SD of paired differences \(\sigma_\Delta\) on \(n\) cells, two-sided at \(\alpha = 0.05\):

\[ \text{SE}(\bar\Delta) \;=\; \frac{\sigma_\Delta}{\sqrt{n}} \qquad\qquad \text{MDE}_{0.05} \;=\; 1.96 \cdot \frac{\sigma_\Delta}{\sqrt{n}} \]

Observed \(\sigma_\Delta \approx 0.58\) for cross-judge differences on the 0-2 scale (n = 109 pooled). Tabulated:

§M8 · The control flow — rubric iteration

The stable process. Given a rubric candidate, deterministically decide promote, revise-with-typed-action, or reject. No vibes.

# INPUT:
#   R_k       — rubric candidate k (hierarchical: 3 top × 3 sub = 9 criteria + 1 penalty)
#   S         — calibration subset, |S| ≥ 30, spans conditions C
#   thresholds — {r2_min: 0.5, collapse_max: 0.85, kappa_min: 0.4,
#                  coverage_min: 0.7, width_max: 0.3}
#   scoring_arch — v1: single-call multi-axis | v2: one call per criterion × cell × judge
# OUTPUT: promoted rubric OR typed rejection report OR architecture-escalation request

function iterate_rubric(R_k, S, thresholds, scoring_arch = v1):

    # Step 0: eye-read disagreement (NEW — missing from §08 Part-C)
    V_prev = run(R_k, S, scoring_arch)
    disagreement_cells = select_top_10_cross_judge_disagreement(V_prev)
    types = human_or_agent_label(disagreement_cells, typology = {
        ANSWER_QUALITY, FRESHNESS_AMBIGUITY, PACKET_INSUFFICIENT,
        RUBRIC_AMBIGUITY, JUDGE_LENIENCY, CONDITION_LEAK, OTHER
    })
    # If > 30% of disagreements are RUBRIC_AMBIGUITY, the criteria need wording work
    # before any diagnostic is informative (diagnostics will be measuring noise).

    # Step 1: run the rubric on the typed criterion tensor (§M2b)
    Z = run_criterion_tensor(R_k, S, conditions = C, judges = J, arch = scoring_arch)
    # Z[q,c,m,k,j] ∈ Ω_k ∪ {⊥_typed}     — typed abstention captured

    # Step 2: compute type-aware diagnostics (§M5 D1–D6)
    r2           = schematic_adherence(Z, R_k)
    collapse     = max_pairwise_corr(Z, R_k, type_aware = True)
    kappas_type  = per_criterion_kappa_by_type(Z)   # κ per criterion, right metric per type
    coverage     = observed_positive_weight(Z) / total_positive_weight(R_k)
    interval_w   = mean(S_max - S_min)
    referenceability_pass = all(ref_tag(k) != low or mode(k) == holistic for k in R_k)

    # Step 3: compute simplified variance (§M3 five-term)
    tau_c        = condition_effect(Z)              # the target estimand
    sigma_γ      = judge_identity_variance(Z)
    sigma_ε      = flicker_variance(Z)

    # Step 4: DECISION
    if all_pass(r2, collapse, kappas_type, coverage, interval_w, referenceability_pass):
        return promote(R_k, scoring_arch)

    # Step 5: typed revision (ONE change per failure)
    if r2 < r2_min:
        action = "tighten criterion wording; require citation contract"
    elif collapse ≥ collapse_max:
        if scoring_arch == v1 and revision_count ≥ 2:
            return escalate("collapse persists across revisions under single-call arch."
                            "promote to scoring-architecture-v2: per-criterion LLM calls")
        action = "merge collapsed pair OR re-scope each"
    elif min(kappas_type) < kappa_min:
        k* = argmin(kappas_type)
        action = "on criterion k*: binarize, add behavioral anchor,"
                  "OR add typed CANNOT_ASSESS"
    elif coverage < coverage_min or interval_w > width_max:
        action = "revise packet (wire probe_metadata.json, bound slice),"
                  "add typed abstention, or drop low-referenceability criterion"
    elif not referenceability_pass:
        k* = lowest_referenceability_atomic_criterion()
        action = "demote k* to holistic OR drop"

    R_{k+1} = revise(R_k, action)
    return iterate_rubric(R_{k+1}, S, thresholds, scoring_arch)

Termination

  • Promote — all three diagnostics pass → \(R_k\) becomes the new baseline.
  • Reject — after a bounded budget (e.g., 4 revisions) without convergence → output the typed report naming the criterion that couldn't be stabilized, and stop. Operator inspects.

Invariants preserved across iterations

  • Judge is uniform across all conditions. Condition is metadata, not a rubric branch.
  • Rubric is uniform across all probes. No per-probe customization.
  • Atomic / holistic flags per sub-criterion do not change mid-iteration — only criterion content changes.
  • Weight signs (which criteria are penalties) are part of the rubric spec; revision may re-scale them but must preserve the sign unless explicitly re-designed.

Input contract

  • Subset \(S\) contains at least 30 cells spanning all conditions in \(C\). Current calibration subset (2026-04-21) satisfies this.
  • At least two judges run on each cell so per-criterion κ and σ_γ are computable.
  • Thresholds (0.5, 0.85, 0.4) are Feuer / Landis-Koch defaults — can be tightened per deployment, cannot be loosened without explicit sign-off.

◆ Why this is the stable process

Every rubric-v2 candidate goes through the same pipeline and lands one of three places: promote, revise-with-action, reject-with-report. The action per failure mode is deterministic. The diagnostics are standard psychometrics. The aggregation is Autorubric. No step relies on a judgment call that isn't a named threshold — which is what makes this auditable and (eventually, §M9) automatable.

§M8c · The open experiment (what this rubric closes)

Zhang 2026 explicitly states (2603.28005, lines 88, 100, 184, 517, 552, 570): "atomic pipelines with externally supplied decompositions or multi-stage extract-then-verify remain untested." Yeadon 2026 confirms holistic essays remain a wall under every conventional rubric. Autorubric achieves κ ≥ 0.64 on referenceable binary with designer-time atomic + few-shot calibration.

The hypothesis the §M8b skeleton tests:

"For a task between plot-marking (Yeadon referenceable regime) and essay-marking (Yeadon wall) — reconstructing an individual's live working state from bounded evidence — does a designer-time atomic rubric over referenceable sub-criteria, combined with a small holistic shell for global-omission detection and a negative-weight fabrication penalty, recover per-criterion κ ≥ 0.5 under a locked judge-bias σ_γ that pure holistic rubrics cannot?"

That sentence is a falsifiable n=1 benchmark result and a paper abstract simultaneously. The "externally-supplied decomposition" Zhang calls open is exactly our 3×3 skeleton. The "essay wall" Yeadon names is our Axis C low-referenceability criteria — we don't pretend to clear it; we mark it with CANNOT_ASSESS and intervals.

The n=1 angle — three concrete failure modes from our data

These are detectable only because of longitudinal single-user context. A cross-population benchmark structurally cannot test them:

  • R08/zero — agent reported DB/adapter meta-state instead of the user's actual March-14 observe-subsystem work. "Wrong world-model" only identifiable because we know this user's trajectory that day.
  • R19 — Syke-cleanup vs LM-Studio thread fork. Both threads are true; which counts as "real last" depends on the user's own sense. No external ground truth.
  • R09 — fabricated UUIDs / thread links look plausible to a generic judge; only caught against this user's actual memex.

The fabrication penalty (§M8b) fires on R09. Axis C holistic criteria fire on R08 and R19. Class C nominal failure typing labels each with its mode. That's the rubric-v2 hypothesis operating on actual cells we have.
